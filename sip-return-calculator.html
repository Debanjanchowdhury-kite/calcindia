SIP Return Calculator: Project Your Mutual Fund SIP Returns

2 July 2026·7 min read

A SIP return calculator answers the single most important question every mutual-fund investor asks: if I invest ₹X per month for Y years, what will I have at the end? This guide explains the formula behind the number, shows realistic return assumptions across fund categories, and covers the two things most SIP return calculators quietly ignore: inflation and LTCG tax.

How a SIP Return Calculator Works

Every honest SIP return calculator uses the future-value-of-annuity formula. Each monthly SIP contribution compounds for the remaining months of your tenure — the first month's ₹5,000 gets full n months of compounding, the last month's ₹5,000 gets just one.

M = P × [((1 + r)^n − 1) / r] × (1 + r)

where:
P = monthly SIP amount
r = monthly return = annual rate ÷ 12
n = number of months

For a ₹10,000 monthly SIP at 12% annual returns over 20 years: r = 0.01, n = 240, and the formula gives you approximately ₹99.9 lakh. Half of that is what you invested; the other half is compound returns.

Realistic SIP Return Rates by Fund Category

The "12%" assumption you see everywhere applies to equity funds over long periods (10+ years). Different fund types have historically delivered different ranges:

Historical returns are not guarantees. To stress-test any SIP return calculator's number, run it at three rates — an optimistic (14%), a realistic (12%), and a pessimistic (8%) — and plan against the pessimistic outcome.

What Most SIP Return Calculators Get Wrong

1. They show nominal returns, not real returns

Your ₹99.9 lakh at year 20 sounds huge — but at 6% inflation, that has the purchasing power of roughly ₹31 lakh in today's rupees. When you plan a goal (₹1 crore for retirement, ₹50 lakh for education), the goal is in today's money. So either inflate the goal or discount the calculator's output:

Real value = Nominal value ÷ (1 + inflation)^years

Our Lumpsum Calculator shows both nominal and inflation-adjusted values — use it as a mental model for how much your SIP corpus is really worth.

2. They ignore LTCG tax

Since Budget 2024, equity mutual fund gains above ₹1.25 lakh per financial year are taxed at 12.5% LTCG. On a ₹50 lakh corpus with ₹35 lakh gain, roughly ₹4.2 lakh goes to tax — so plan for around 8–9% of your gains being trimmed at withdrawal, not zero.

Debt funds are worse: taxed at your income slab rate (no indexation for units bought after April 2023). If you're in the 30% bracket, that's a big drag on debt-fund SIP returns.

3. They assume you never stop

A 20-year projection assumes you keep depositing every single month, through every market crash, salary loss and life event. Real investors pause SIPs on average 3–4 times in a 20-year run. Model your SIP return with that in mind — either use conservative assumptions, or set up an annual step-up that offsets missed months in later years.

SIP Return vs Fixed Deposit — The Real Post-Tax Comparison

People compare a 12% SIP return to a 7% FD return and see 5% extra. Post-tax, the gap is smaller than that: a 12% equity SIP after LTCG works out to roughly 10.5% effective. A 7% FD, taxed at 30% slab, is only 4.9% effective. So the real gap is closer to 5.6%, not the raw 5% headline. See our SIP vs Fixed Deposit calculator for the full side-by-side.

How to Actually Use a SIP Return Calculator

  1. Start with the goal, not the SIP amount. "I want ₹1 crore in 20 years in today's money" — inflated at 6%, that's ₹3.2 crore nominal. Now solve for the SIP.
  2. Run three return scenarios (8%, 10%, 12%). Plan against the middle.
  3. Add a 10% step-up. Match your SIP increment to your annual salary hike. Uses our Step-Up SIP Calculator.
  4. Subtract LTCG tax from the target. Aim for goal + ~10% buffer.
  5. Re-check every year. Life, income, and markets all change; your assumptions should too.
Bottom line: A SIP return calculator is a projection tool, not a promise. The formula is exact; the return rate is a guess. The best users treat the calculator's output as a range, plan against the low end, and adjust every year based on how their actual portfolio is performing.
